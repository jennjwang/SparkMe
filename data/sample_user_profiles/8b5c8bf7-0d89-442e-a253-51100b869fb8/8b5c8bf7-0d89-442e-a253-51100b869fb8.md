Communication preferences vary with task complexity: conversational AI interfaces are favored for brainstorming and multi-step problem solving, while command-based tools are employed for repetitive, specific tasks such as data formatting.

### Trust and Control Over AI

Given the specialized, tacit knowledge required in clinical consulting, including strict healthcare regulations and compliance mandates, the individual uses AI tools as augmentation rather than replacement of domain expertise. AI is trusted for drafting and preliminary data analysis but not for final clinical recommendations or nuanced regulatory interpretations. They treat AI outputs as starting points, applying rigorous expert review to all deliverables to ensure accuracy. The preferred balance leans toward human-led analysis for complex, client-facing documents, while supporting 50 to 70 percent automation for repetitive reporting and formatting tasks. High automation is acceptable for internal reporting and data aggregation but is considered potentially threatening if it risks regulatory compliance or client trust. Consequently, robust audit trails and manual override capabilities are mandatory for any automated process.

Privacy and data security concerns are prominent, motivating advocacy for robust safeguards.
